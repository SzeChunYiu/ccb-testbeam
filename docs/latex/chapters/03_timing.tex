\chapter{Timing}

\section{Scope and observables}

The timing program asks how much time resolution can be extracted from the
eighteen-sample HRD waveform without using Monte Carlo truth or event labels that
are algebraically derived from the quantity being predicted.  The central
observable is a same-particle pair residual between two downstream B-stack
staves,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_{ij,e} =
  \left(t_{j,e} - x_j/v\right) -
  \left(t_{i,e} - x_i/v\right),
  \qquad v^{-1}=0.078~{\rm ns/cm},
\end{equation}
where the geometry term removes the expected time of flight for the stave
spacing.  Resolution is quoted as the central 68 percent half-width,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{68}(\Delta t) =
  \frac{Q_{84}(\Delta t)-Q_{16}(\Delta t)}{2},
\end{equation}
with bootstrap confidence intervals.  The quoted pair widths are deliberately
not converted into a single detector number unless the covariance assumptions
are under discussion: the S05 studies show that the pair residuals are not
independent and that the B2-containing topology dominates much of the residual
covariance.

All headline comparisons are split by run.  In the initial B-stack timing
benchmark the training runs were 58--63 and the held-out run was 65.  Later
stability studies use leave-one-run-out folds across Sample-II analysis runs.
The data gate was reproduced from raw ROOT before each timing study; for example,
the S02 and S19a reproductions both recover the 640737 selected B-stave pulses
and the Sample-II per-stave counts exactly.  This matters because the timing
metrics are sensitive to selector semantics and to the sparse all-three
downstream subset.

\section{Pickoff and timewalk}

The first benchmark compared conventional pickoffs--leading edge, constant
fraction discriminator (CFD), optimum-filter windows, and template phase--against
a run-split ridge residual corrector.  The strongest conventional pickoff in
S02 was a template-phase estimator.  The ridge model used same-pulse waveform
features only: normalized samples, log amplitude, peak sample, area over
amplitude, and stave indicators.  Its target was the CFD20 residual relative to
the other downstream staves in the same event; no held-out run identifiers,
event identifiers, event order, or other-stave times were included as features.

\midrule

On this first held-out test, the ridge correction narrowed the pairwise
\(\sigma_{68}\) by about 1.04 ns relative to the best fixed traditional scan.
That result established that the raw CFD/template times still contained
waveform-dependent residual structure.  It did not by itself establish that the
structure required a black-box model: the target is an inter-stave consistency
proxy, and the held-out set contains only 66 all-three downstream events.

The follow-up timewalk studies made that distinction explicit.  The analytic
model writes the corrected time as
\begin{equation}
  t'_i =
  t^{\rm phase}_i -
  \left[
    \beta_{0,i} + \beta_{1}\log A_i
    + \beta_{2} A_i^{-1}
    + \beta_{3} A_i^{-1/2}
  \right],
\end{equation}
where \(A_i\) is the same-pulse amplitude and the stave intercepts absorb
constant channel offsets.  The selected S03a family was the amplitude-only ridge
form with \(\alpha=100\), trained only on runs 58--63.  It reduced the run-65
template-phase width from 2.889 ns to 1.495 ns.  A ridge model on template-phase
residuals reached a slightly narrower point estimate, 1.392 ns, but the
interpretation changed: most of the S02 ridge gain was a physics-like amplitude
timewalk correction that a transparent conventional model could represent.

useful evidence for residual nonlinear structure; it is not by itself a
production timing replacement.

\section{Deep timing models}

The P03 studies tested whether the normalized waveform samples contain timing
information beyond the analytic timewalk model.  P03a used a small
heteroskedastic MLP with 18 normalized waveform samples plus a stave one-hot
intercept.  P03b repeated the comparison in leave-one-run-out folds.  P03c
added a two-layer one-dimensional CNN and trained both neural models on the
residuals left by the analytic baseline.  These models explicitly excluded run
number, event id, event order, other-stave timing, pair residuals, and held-out
targets; shuffled-target controls were run as negative controls.

The first P03 conclusion was negative for deep timing.  Across seven held-out
runs, the MLP beat the earlier S02 ridge reference on several folds but did not
beat the analytic timewalk baseline.  On the run-65 residual-target benchmark,
the MLP point estimate was 1.448 ns and the CNN was 1.497 ns; the small
MLP improvement over the analytic 1.495 ns had a paired delta interval that
included zero.  The CNN added essentially nothing.

The S19a result is a useful update, but it should be read as an architecture

single held-out architecture sweep: a neural winner must keep its advantage
under run-family transfer, event-block controls, and support-preserving
augmentation tests.

\section{Same-particle covariance and two-ended resolution}

Same-particle timing resolution is not just a one-dimensional residual-width
problem.  If pair residuals are written as
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\Delta t}_e = L \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_e,
  \qquad
  {\rm Cov}(\boldsymbol{\Delta t}) = L \Sigma_{\rm stave} L^{T},
\end{equation}
then off-diagonal pair covariances constrain the stave covariance matrix
\(\Sigma_{\rm stave}\).  S05c used this relation to test whether the B-stack
pair residuals behave like independent stave errors.  They do not.  The
traditional pair-median residuals have much larger B2-containing covariance than
downstream-only covariance, and the stave decomposition assigns the largest
excess variance to B2.

The ML residual model reduces width, with a run-bootstrap
\(\Delta\sigma_{68}=-0.633\) ns relative to the pair-median baseline and a
95 percent interval of [-8.271, -0.451] ns.  However, the covariance result is
the more important timing-systematics message.  In the raw pair-median baseline,
the mean absolute covariance for B2-containing pair pairs is
1042 ns\({}^2\) [748, 1312], whereas downstream-only pair pairs are
16.0 ns\({}^2\) [4.76, 37.1].  ML reduces the covariance scale but does not turn
the detector into an independent-stave system.  Timing resolutions quoted from
pair residuals therefore need topology and covariance qualifiers.

S04e shows that q-template policies are most useful as B2/topology pathology
vetoes.  The RF q-template veto has the narrowest B2-containing point estimate
and reduces the \(>5\) ns tail fraction to 0.0053, but it does so after rejecting
about 44 percent of B2-containing pair rows; the correct use is therefore a
support map, not a universal timing correction.  S06a asks whether better charge
or depth proxy information monotonically improves timing resolution.  It does
not: the charge-aware ridge model is broader than the hierarchical traditional
timewalk by 0.125 ns, with a delta interval [0.072, 0.198] ns, and the matched
charge slopes remain positive.  Higher charge is not a simple route to a
narrower timing table after the matched controls.

S05a tested whether event-matched A-stack controls remove a common B-stack
timing component.  They did not: adding A controls to the traditional ridge
model had a bootstrap delta of approximately [-0.017, 0.017] ns, and the
runwise-shuffled A-control check was essentially indistinguishable from the
nominal A-control result.  S05d and S05e then converted the covariance picture
into two-ended priors and saturation-aware variants.  These studies show useful
ML narrowing in some projections, but the robust conclusion is that the B-pulse
topology and local waveform state dominate the same-particle covariance.

\section{A-stack cross-checks}

The A-stack provides an independent timing telescope and a useful guard against
overinterpreting B-stack-specific effects.  S18 reproduced the A1--A3 timing
width from raw A-stack ROOT.  On Sample III, the conventional CFD20 polynomial
timewalk model gave a robust width of 1.389 ns [1.339, 1.453], while a ridge
timewalk model gave 1.383 ns [1.337, 1.426].  The difference is negligible.  On
Sample IV, the robust width was broader, but the sample contains only 127 pairs
and is sensitive to calibration pool and estimator choices.

S18d explains much of the apparent Sample-IV broadening.  The historical
binned-Gaussian core-sigma excess is unstable under estimator and fit-window
changes.  Under a run-held-out traditional correction, the Sample-IV
binned-Gaussian width is 2.077 ns with an upper bootstrap bound at the fit limit,
but the Student-\(t\) scale is 1.240 ns [1.058, 1.368] and the percentile-68
width is 1.506 ns [1.296, 1.683].  Across robust unbinned estimators, the
Sample-IV minus Sample-III excess is about 0.09 ns rather than the historical
0.54 ns binned-core excess.  S18e further shows that the best traditional
calibration pool, late Sample III, gives 1.457 ns [1.242, 1.639] on Sample IV,
while the corresponding ML pool degrades to 2.262 ns.  The A-stack therefore
supports the main timing message: transparent timewalk calibration is already
near the measurable resolution floor, and many apparent improvements or
broadenings are calibration-pool or estimator effects.

\section{Systematics and caveats}

Several systematic limits recur across the timing reports.  First, the main
same-particle timing target is a residual proxy built from other staves, not an
external time truth.  A model can reduce pair width by correcting a real timing
effect, by learning correlated detector state, or by accidentally coupling to
the proxy target.  This is why the reports require run splits, feature audits,
and shuffled-target sentinels.

Second, the all-three downstream subset is sparse on the most important held-out
run: S02, S03a, P03c, and S19a each have 66 events and 198 pair residuals on run
65.  Event-level bootstrap intervals quantify the finite held-out distribution,
but they do not cover all model-selection uncertainty.  Leave-one-run-out and
run-bootstrap summaries are consequently more reliable for production
interpretation than a single run-65 point estimate.

Third, a narrow Gaussian core is not a sufficient timing claim.  The reports
quote \(\sigma_{68}\), full RMS, tail fractions, and core-fit diagnostics because
template-phase residuals can have a narrow fitted core and broad structured
tails.  The operational timing model must improve the robust width without
creating tails or relying on a fit-window artifact.

Finally, the B-stack covariance is topology-dependent.  B2-containing residuals
carry much larger covariance than downstream-only residuals, and A-stack
controls do not cleanly remove this component.  Any downstream physics use of
the timing resolution should propagate a topology/covariance systematic rather
than assuming \(\sigma^2_{ij}=\sigma_i^2+\sigma_j^2\) with independent staves.

\section{Chapter conclusion}

The timing studies identify three different ``winners,'' depending on the
decision standard.  For the initial S02 pickoff benchmark, ridge residual
correction on CFD20 wins over the best fixed traditional template-phase scan.
For the conservative timing calibration, the analytic amplitude timewalk is the
preferred traditional winner: it explains most of the ridge gain, transfers
stably across run folds, and remains interpretable.  For the S19a architecture
screen, a small MLP is the held-out point-estimate ML/NN winner, reaching
1.176 ns on run 65 versus 1.495 ns for the analytic baseline, while the 1D-CNN,
attention, TCN, GRU, ResNet, ridge, and gradient-boosted tree variants provide
useful but not yet production-grade support.

The physics conclusion is therefore not that deeper networks are unnecessary in
all timing tasks, but that this short, phase-locked waveform is already well
described by amplitude timewalk plus low-dimensional pulse-shape terms.  ML is a
powerful residual diagnostic and can win point-estimate architecture screens;
the adopted timing interpretation remains governed by run stability, leakage
controls, covariance topology, and estimator robustness.

\chapter{Introduction and experimental setup}

\section{Physics context}

This report summarises a data-driven analysis of a proton test-beam data set
recorded at the Cyclotron Centre Bronowice (CCB) in Krakow.  A
\SI{190}{MeV} proton beam impinged on a deuterated polyethylene target
(CD$_2$) near the entrance of the detector system.  Charged reaction products
then crossed trigger scintillators, a time projection chamber, and two HRD
scintillator range stacks.  The detector was operated as a range telescope:
penetration depth, pulse amplitude, pulse shape, and inter-stave timing jointly
encode the sample-level particle topology.

The analysis deliberately avoids per-event truth claims.  No Monte Carlo truth
labels are available for the primary data set.  Consequently, particle identity
and energy statements are interpreted at the sample or topology level, while
per-pulse claims are tied to reproducible waveform observables and held-out-run
closure tests.  The two physics questions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item how precisely the HRD staves timestamp the same charged particle, and
  \item when overlapping pulses, baseline motion, and saturation corrupt the
  timing or charge reconstruction.
\end{enumerate}
These questions are coupled: the same waveform features that sharpen timing
also diagnose pile-up, saturation, and pedestal pathologies.

\section{Detector layout}

The HRD system contains two scintillator range stacks, denoted Stack A and
Stack B, positioned approximately \SI{100}{cm} from the target.  The B-stack is
the main analysis detector.  Its usable physical staves are B2, B4, B6, and
B8, corresponding to the even raw waveform channel blocks.  The A-stack
provides an independent cross-check through the usable A1 and A3 staves; A2
and A4 are not used in the selected-pulse analysis.  Each stave is roughly
\SI{1}{m} long and is read out at one end through a wavelength-shifting fibre.
The propagation speed used in the reconstruction notes is
$v_{\mathrm{WLS}}=\SI{17.0}{cm/ns}$.

For a pulse on stave $i$, the nominal propagation-corrected time is written
\begin{equation}
  t'_{i,e}=t_{i,e}-\frac{x_i}{v},
  \label{eq:intro-propagation}
\end{equation}
where $e$ indexes the event, $x_i$ is the stave depth coordinate, and the
effective inverse velocity used by the run-held-out timing studies is
$v^{-1}=0.078~\mathrm{ns/cm}$.  The B-stack geometry is treated as a systematic
because the older notes and newer reports differ on whether the relevant
centre-to-centre spacing is approximately \SI{2}{cm} or \SI{4}{cm}; count
reproduction is unaffected, but timing-scale interpretations retain this
geometry caveat.

\section{Waveforms and pulse selection}

The primitive object in the analysis is an 18-sample waveform $y_{e,i,k}$, with
sample index $k=0,\ldots,17$.  The production B-stack pulse selection is a
deterministic threshold on the pedestal-subtracted peak:
\begin{align}
  b_{e,i} &= \mathrm{median}\{y_{e,i,0},y_{e,i,1},y_{e,i,2},y_{e,i,3}\},\\
  A_{e,i} &= \max_k\left(y_{e,i,k}-b_{e,i}\right),\\
  \mathrm{selected}_{e,i} &\Longleftrightarrow A_{e,i}>1000~\mathrm{ADC}.
  \label{eq:intro-selection}
\end{align}
Applying Eq.~\eqref{eq:intro-selection} to the even raw HRDv channel blocks
reproduces the selected B-stave table exactly.  This exact reproduction is the
first gate for all downstream studies because sorted or derived amplitude
branches have different semantics and can silently change the population.

\section{Traditional and machine-learning methods}

All benchmark studies follow the same discipline: reproduce the relevant raw
number first, split by run, train only on non-held-out runs, and quote
bootstrap intervals on the held-out metric.  A model is treated as a physics
candidate only when it beats a strong conventional method on the pre-registered
metric and survives leakage checks such as forbidden-feature audits,
event-overlap tests, and shuffled-target controls.

The principal conventional methods are deterministic thresholding, empirical
amplitude-binned templates, analytic amplitude timewalk corrections, bounded
two-pulse template fits, and Poisson occupancy models.  The ML/NN comparators
include ridge regression, calibrated logistic models, gradient-boosted trees,
MLPs, 1D-CNNs, and a small architecture sweep with ResNet, TCN, attention, and
GRU variants.  The same-particle residual target used by the timing studies is
\begin{equation}
  r_{i,e}=t'_{i,e,\mathrm{base}}
  -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j\ne i}t'_{j,e,\mathrm{base}},
  \label{eq:intro-residual}
\end{equation}
where only same-pulse waveform and stave features are allowed into residual
models.  For pile-up rate estimates, the reference occupancy model is
\begin{equation}
  \mu = R\,\tau_{\mathrm{eff}},
  \qquad
  R_{\max}=\frac{\mu_{\max}}{\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}},
  \label{eq:intro-poisson}
\end{equation}
so any revision to the effective live-time directly rescales the admissible
rate.

\section{Headline physics orientation}

The strongest timing baseline after calibration is not a neural network but an
analytic amplitude/timewalk correction.  It reduces the run-65 pairwise
sigma68 from the template-phase value of about \SI{2.89}{ns} to about
\SI{1.50}{ns}.  Small ML residual correctors can improve the point estimate,
but many such improvements are control-sensitive.  The stable interpretation
is that the 18-sample waveform is low-dimensional and strongly constrained by
amplitude and phase.

Pile-up behaves differently.  The original rate estimate with
$\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}=\SI{90}{ns}$ gives $R_{\max}\simeq\SI{4.22}{MHz}$, but a
direct timing-template live-time measurement finds a 10\% crossing of
$\SI{124.79}{ns}$ with 95\% CI [123.33, 126.36] ns.  Substituting this value
into Eq.~\eqref{eq:intro-poisson} gives
$R_{\max}\simeq\SI{3.05}{MHz}$, so the rate limit is materially lower than the
assumption-based headline.  ML scores are useful monitoring variables for
current-dependent waveform changes, but the physics-facing rate handle remains
the transparent topology and live-time model.

\section{Systematics and caveats}

Several limitations shape the rest of the report.  First, all conclusions are
conditioned on the raw-HRDv waveform gate in Eq.~\eqref{eq:intro-selection};
sorted or derived branches can reproduce similar-looking quantities while
selecting a different population.  Second, per-event energy and particle-ID
truth are absent in the data-driven studies.  The range-energy relation
$R(T)=aT^p$, with $a=1.913\times10^{-3}$ and $p=1.797$, is an interpretive
scale anchored to stopping-power points, not a replacement for a calibrated
GEANT4 truth model.  Third, the one-ended WLS readout and the B-stack spacing
ambiguity propagate into timing-scale systematics.  Fourth, injected two-pulse
and artificial-saturation benchmarks provide controlled truth, but their
support need not cover all high-current or naturally saturated waveforms.

Finally, ML superiority is not treated as transitive across tasks.  Gradient
boosted trees and compact neural networks win several closure benchmarks where
the signal is genuinely in waveform shape, such as saturation recovery and
injected two-pulse timing.  They tie or lose when a physics-motivated analytic
model already captures the dominant structure, as in timewalk and Poisson
rate modelling.  This distinction is the organising principle for the
chapters that follow.
